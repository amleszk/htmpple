The moon Iapetus might've had an ancient ring system that formed its equatorial ridge. A ring system can come from a moon as the moon crosses the Roche Limit of the object it is orbiting. Mars' moon Phobos may break up this way and form a ring system around the planet in only millions of years.

Any spacecraft that humanity sent to orbit the moon and the people that orbited it can be considered as the moon's own moon. I don't know what the people would be called, but the spacecraft are/were the moon's own artificial satellites. Even some asteroids have their own moons.

It is possible to orbit a moon and has been done, but it is much more difficult to orbit it than its planet because the planet has a much greater mass. A spacecraft would require many flybys or engine burns to slow it down so it's captured by a moon. Carrying the extra fuel is very cost prohibitive. MESSENGER took a long time to become the first spacecraft to orbit Mercury for similar reasons. JUICE will become an artificial satellite of one of Jupiter's moons.

tl;dr: Orbiting a moon is definitely possible, but it's much easier to orbit something nearby (a planet) with much greater mass and a much larger Hill sphere.
